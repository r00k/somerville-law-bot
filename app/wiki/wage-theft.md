Somerville's wage theft ordinance targets employers who commit "wage theft" — defined in Sec. 9-34 as failing to make timely or complete wage payments, failing to pay minimum or prevailing wage, or failing to pay earned overtime. The city's main tool is leverage over licenses, permits, and contracts rather than direct prosecution. Under Sec. 9-31, the city may deny, revoke, or suspend a license or permit if the applicant admitted or was found guilty/liable in the prior five years of violating listed state or federal wage laws (M.G.L. c. 149, c. 151, and federal law). A denied or revoked applicant cannot get a license or permit for one year (Sec. 9-31(c)), and applicants must certify a clean record (Sec. 9-31(e)). Denials/revocations can be appealed within 14 days by certified mail, with a hearing within 30 days (Sec. 9-31(d)).

A Wage Theft Advisory Committee (Sec. 9-35) — nine members including union, nonprofit, and business representatives — reviews complaints, coordinates with the Attorney General, and does outreach. Importantly, the city itself does not adjudicate wage claims: under the complaint process (Sec. 9-36), the committee directs complainants to the Attorney General, Department of Labor, or other agencies, then tracks the outcome. So if you're owed wages, this ordinance routes you to state/federal enforcers rather than paying you directly.

Additional rules apply to city contractors (Sec. 9-37, including possible wage bonds of at least $25,000 and rejection of debarred bidders), licensees under M.G.L. c. 138/140 (Sec. 9-38, including a $300/day fine for failing to post a required violation notice, plus a conciliation option), tax increment financing recipients (Sec. 9-39), and municipal construction contracts (Sec. 9-40, with escalating debarment: six months, three years, then permanent). Sanctions also follow "successor" employers sharing an officer and trade (Sec. 9-41).

Separately, the Living Wage Ordinance (Sec. 2-397 through 2-403) requires the city and its service contractors/subcontractors to pay covered employees a living wage (started at $15.00/hour, adjusted annually — Sec. 2-399). Employees can file confidential complaints (Sec. 2-402), retaliation is barred, and remedies include $500/week per-employee fines and contract termination (Sec. 2-403). Note the living wage only covers city work above dollar thresholds and can be waived by exception (Sec. 2-401).

The provided text does not set out a process for the city to recover unpaid wages directly for a private-sector worker — that runs through state/federal agencies.
