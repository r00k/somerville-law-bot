Keeping honeybees (Apis mellifera) in Somerville is regulated by the Board of Health under Appendix D. You cannot keep bees on your property without a Board of Health permit — the fee is $50 for the first year and $25 for annual renewal if there are no changes to your plan or keeper (coo:2177, item 1).

Key rules (all from coo:2177):
- **Limits:** A maximum of 2 colonies per lot (beekeepers already keeping bees when the ordinance passed may be exempted). Each hive can be no more than 5 feet tall and 20 cubic feet (items 2, 6).
- **Neighbors:** You must notify abutters (adjacent properties) of your request to keep bees (item 3). Hives within 20 feet of a principal building on an abutting lot, or on a side yard abutting a street, need a flyway barrier at least 6 feet high — a wall, fence, or dense vegetation running at least 25 feet — that also meets the Zoning Code (item 7).
- **Upkeep:** Keep a constant supply of fresh water (no stagnant, mosquito-breeding water), keep yards free of tall weeds and rotting produce, and don't let the apiary become a public nuisance (items 4, 8, 12b).
- **State law:** You must comply with Massachusetts rules (330 CMR 8.00), including notifying the Commonwealth within 48 hours of getting hives, marking hives with the owner's name, and covering bees when moving them (item 5).
- **Selling honey:** Sales must follow the Mass. DPH Food Protection Program for unprocessed honey, and soil testing is required before any sales, with results posted for buyers (items 9, 12a).
- **Training:** The beekeeper must watch the Board of Health's video on beekeeping in Somerville (item 10).

The Board of Health may suspend, revoke, or decline to renew a permit for cause after notice, and may order a hive removed at the owner's expense if it causes disturbances or damage (items 11, 12). Apiculture is also treated as an accessory use under the Zoning Ordinance's Urban Agriculture category (§ 9.2.14.c.i), so zoning rules may apply in addition to these health regulations.
