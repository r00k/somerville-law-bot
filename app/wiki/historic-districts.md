Somerville regulates changes to buildings and structures in designated **local historic districts**, plus maintenance of "significant" properties citywide. The article's purpose is to protect and preserve the city's historical and architectural resources (Sec. 7-16). An "exterior architectural feature" means any part of a building visible from a public street, way, park, or body of water — including materials, colors, windows, doors, signs, and fixtures (Sec. 7-17).

**Certificate required before work.** Within a historic district, you generally cannot construct or alter anything affecting exterior architectural features — or get a building or demolition permit — until the Historic Preservation Commission issues a certificate of appropriateness, nonapplicability, or hardship (Sec. 7-21(b)). You apply to the Commission with plans and specifications. The Commission weighs historic and architectural value, design, materials, and how the work fits the surroundings (Sec. 7-21(a)). It reviews applications within 14 days, holds a public hearing (with 14-day notice to abutters), and decides within 45 days of filing; if it misses that deadline, a certificate of hardship is automatically issued (Sec. 7-22). Certificates are valid one year (Sec. 7-21(d)(4)).

**Common exceptions.** The Commission does *not* review interior work, temporary structures/signs, storm doors and windows, screens, window air conditioners, antennae, paint color, small signs, or storm-damage reconstruction rebuilt within a year (Sec. 7-21(c)). Ordinary maintenance, repair, or replacement that does not change design, material, color, or appearance is also allowed without review, as is landscaping and urgent public-safety work (Sec. 7-24).

**Minimum maintenance.** Owners of property in a local historic district, on the National Register, or deemed "significant" must not let significant exterior features deteriorate (Sec. 7-29(c)). Note the exclusion: this maintenance standard does **not** apply to properties receiving a residential exemption (Sec. 7-29(a)). Violations can bring an order to repair, a $100/day fine, and even city entry with costs assessed as a lien (Sec. 7-29(f), (g)). Replacing siding is not prohibited, and restoration to original condition is not required (Sec. 7-29(c), (d)).

**Appeals & enforcement.** Commission decisions on certificates can be appealed to South Middlesex Superior Court within 20 days (Sec. 7-23); minimum-maintenance findings are appealed to the Planning Board within 10 business days (Sec. 7-29(e)). Violations of the article carry fines under Sec. 1-11 and can be enforced by court injunction (Sec. 7-26, 7-27).

To check whether a specific address is in a district, see the property table in Sec. 7-18. Districts are created or changed by two-thirds City Council vote after study and hearings (Sec. 7-18, 7-20). See also related topics on demolition review and zoning.
