Somerville regulates demolition through several overlapping rules. The main one is the **Demolition Review Ordinance (Sec. 7-28)**, which protects historically or architecturally "significant" buildings outside local historic districts. "Demolition" here means pulling down, removing, or destroying 50% or more of a building's exterior surface (walls and roof), per the definition in Sec. 7-28(b) and the zoning glossary. Routine maintenance, siding/roof/window replacement, interior renovations, exploratory/interior demolition that doesn't compromise structure, and defined "minor projects" (like adding a dormer, addition, porch, or reshaping a roof) are **not** demolition.

A building is presumptively "significant" if it's on (or proposed for) the National Register, or is at least 75 years old and found significant by the Historic Preservation Commission (Sec. 7-28(c),(e)). When you apply for a demolition, building, or zoning permit that would demolish such a building, ISD notifies the Commission within 10 days (Sec. 7-28(d)). The Commission then makes an **initial determination** of significance at a public hearing, with notice to abutters within 300 feet and multilingual on-site posting (Sec. 7-28(e)). If significant, a **final determination** hearing decides whether it's "preferably preserved" (Sec. 7-28(f)). If so, the building official cannot issue a demolition permit for up to **18 months** — the demolition review period — during which owners must pursue alternatives (Sec. 7-28(g)). The Commission can shorten this period, or it can expire, allowing demolition. Determinations are valid three years before the process restarts.

**Gotchas:** Exempt areas (Assembly Square, Brickbottom, Inner Belt, Boynton Yards, certain Union Square/Grand Junction blocks, local historic districts, and city-owned buildings) skip this review (Sec. 7-28, exempt-areas definition). "Demolition by neglect" — deliberately letting a building decay — counts as voluntary demolition and is enforceable. Illegal demolition carries $300/day fines, possible municipal liens, reconstruction orders, and building-permit delays (Sec. 7-28(h)). Emergency demolitions of unsafe structures may proceed under state law (Sec. 7-28(j)(2)).

Separately, **Sec. 11-105** sets permit conditions for physically demolishing a building: fire details for structures within 30 feet of another, a dust-control plan, and **certified-mail notice to abutters and owners within 300 feet (100 feet for very small structures) at least seven days before the permit issues**, plus posting on the property and in City Hall. Emergency demolitions can waive notice (Sec. 11-105(c)(6)).

Finally, **Sec. 8-375–8-381** require a site safety plan approved by ISD and a designated OSHA-10-certified site safety coordinator before any demolition permit issues, except homeowner-exempt work.

For related rules see local historic districts (Sec. 7-18) and fire details (§§ 5-8, 5-36).
