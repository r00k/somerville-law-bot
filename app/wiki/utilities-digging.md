Anyone who wants to dig up or open a street, sidewalk, or other public place in Somerville needs a written **street opening permit** first (Sec. 11-143). Permits for laying or repairing drains come from the city engineer; all other openings come from the commissioner of public works. The permit must be kept on site and shown to police on demand. Before a permit issues, the applicant must notify abutters within 300 feet by certified mail at least seven days ahead, post notice at the site and in city hall for seven days, and file an affidavit of compliance (Sec. 11-143(b)). These notice rules can be waived only for a genuine emergency threatening public health and safety (Sec. 11-143(c)).

The technical rules for openings in asphalt are in Sec. 12-20: a $50 nonrefundable application fee, a $500 refundable deposit, a $5,000 performance bond, and a $500,000 liability insurance certificate. You must notify the highway superintendent 24 hours ahead and call Dig Safe. A major gotcha — the permittee stays responsible for any settlement of the opening for **three years** after the final infra-red permanent repair, and the city bills you for corrections (Sec. 12-20(7), (11)).

Related requirements: gas pipes, sewers, conduits, poles, and similar structures under or over a street need a location plan approved by the city engineer (Sec. 11-142); vaults and coal-holes need city council permission (Sec. 11-144). Excavations must be guarded with railings and lights (Sec. 11-84), and the superintendent may require a security bond before you dig (Sec. 11-85). Drain and sewer work into a common sewer must be done by a licensed drain layer who posts a $10,000 bond and gets a permit from the city engineer (Sec. 8-41, 8-42, 8-43).

Two important restrictions: a newly reconstructed or resurfaced street generally cannot be opened for **at least five years** except in certified actual necessity (Sec. 11-82), and closing a travel lane or sidewalk for work more than eight hours requires Traffic Director approval and a traffic management plan (Appendix E, Sec. 8-3). Utility gas companies must report leaks and repair schedules to public works (Sec. 11-126).

These sections don't list a single consolidated fee schedule beyond Sec. 12-20, and they don't spell out homeowner-versus-contractor distinctions for who may apply — check with public works for your specific situation. See related topics on driveways/paving (Sec. 11-88) and utility poles and wires (Sec. 11-55 through 11-68).
