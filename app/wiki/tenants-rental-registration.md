Somerville's housing rules here focus mostly on landlord notice and disclosure duties rather than a general rental-unit registry. Note there is no active rent control in these provisions.

**Posting owner/agent info (Sec. 7-1).** A landlord who rents residential space but doesn't live in the building (and has no resident manager) must post a durable notice at least 20 square inches near the mailboxes or entry area, showing the owner's (or managing trustee/partner/corporate president's) and any agent's name, address, and phone. The notice must also tell tenants they can request a code inspection through the City's Constituent Services Department. Violations are enforced under Sec. 1-11(b), with each day a separate offense.

**Housing Stability Notification Act (Secs. 7-280–7-287).** This applies to nearly all rental units in the city (Sec. 7-282), with exceptions for hospital/nursing/health facilities and short-term substance-abuse treatment housing. At the start of any new tenancy (written or oral), the landlord must give the tenant a "basic tenancy rights and resources" notice by the fifth day (Sec. 7-283(1)). When serving a notice to quit or notice of lease non-renewal/expiration, the landlord or foreclosing owner must simultaneously provide a housing-rights-and-resources notice, and must give at least 30 days' notice before starting a summary-process (eviction) case for non-renewal (Sec. 7-283(2)). Notices must go in-hand or by certified mail, in the tenant's primary language (Spanish, Portuguese, Haitian Creole, or Nepali) if the owner knows it. These rights cannot be waived by lease (Sec. 7-284), and enforcement is through non-criminal fines under Sec. 1-11, with each affected tenant a separate offense (Sec. 7-286).

**Condominium/cooperative conversion.** Definitions in Sec. 7-63 govern conversion evictions, including protections tied to elderly, disabled, and low/moderate-income tenants; the full conversion procedures and timelines are only partly shown here.

**Gotchas.** The nuisance ordinance (Sec. 9-97) can hold property owners liable for repeat "public nuisance" gatherings, but not if the owner is actively pursuing eviction of the responsible tenant. "Rental registration" as a term appears only for *vacant* property (Sec. 11-111), which requires owners to register within 90 days of vacancy and pay an annual fee — that's a separate obligation, not a general tenant registry. These sections don't set out full eviction procedures, security-deposit rules, or habitability standards; those are largely state law.

*This summary is for general orientation only. The cited section text above is authoritative — always verify anything that matters against the linked sections, not this summary.*
