This ordinance (the "Somerville Condominium/Cooperative Conversion Ordinance," Sec. 7-62) regulates converting rental apartments into condominiums or cooperatives. "Convert" means the initial offer to sell a unit as a condo or co-op (Sec. 7-63). It applies to "housing accommodations," which excludes single-family homes, government-owned/operated housing, dorms, nursing homes, and short-term lodging (Sec. 7-63) — though a single-family becomes covered if additional units are added.

Notice requirements depend on occupancy (Sec. 7-64(a)). For vacant or owner-occupied buildings, an owner must give two years' advance notice to the Condominium Review Board, with several exceptions (long-term owner or waiver-signing non-rent-paying occupant, perpetually deed-restricted affordable units, or sale to a close family member). For occupied units, tenants and the Board get written notice (translated if needed), triggering key protections: tenants generally have one year before they must vacate, but elderly, disabled, or low/moderate-income tenants get five years (Sec. 7-64(a)(2)).

Tenants get an exclusive right to purchase their own unit at fair market "as-is" value, for 120 days (180 for protected tenants) (Sec. 7-64(d)). There's no tenant right to purchase for sales to relatives within the fourth degree of kindred, or when an innocent buyer brings a non-permitted conversion into compliance (unless they own more than two units).

Tenants who don't buy and relocate receive relocation payments — $18,000 for elderly/disabled/low-moderate income tenants and $14,000 for others (as of October 1, 2025), adjusted annually by CPI (Sec. 7-64(e)). Owners must also help protected tenants find comparable Somerville housing during the five-year period, unless exempt for keeping rents low (Sec. 7-64(f)). During the notice period, rent increases are capped (CPI or 10%, whichever is less), tenants keep quiet enjoyment, and owners may only make code-required repairs in occupied units (Sec. 7-64(g), (h)).

A "condominium conversion eviction" is barred until the notice period expires, and non-compliance is a defense in eviction court (Sec. 7-64(c)). The five-member Review Board (two homeowners, two tenants, one protected-status member) runs hearings and issues conditional/preliminary and final permits (Sec. 7-65, Sec. 7-67); no final permit issues until the tenant vacates or the notice period ends. Violations carry fines of at least $1,000 per offense, and tenants may pursue civil remedies (Sec. 7-71).

Note: the burden of proving elderly/disabled/low-moderate-income status falls on the tenant if contested; the Office of Housing Stability can assist (Sec. 7-64(a)(3)). For eviction protections generally, see related tenant/housing topics.
