This topic covers two related duties: clearing snow and ice from sidewalks, and keeping streets clear for plows during snow emergencies.

**Clearing your sidewalk (Sec. 12-8).** Anyone in charge of land or a building — owner, occupant, tenant, or agent — must not leave snow or ice on the abutting sidewalk for more than six hours between sunrise and sunset after a weather event ends (the city can extend this at its discretion). You must clear the sidewalk down to the surface, or treat it with sand or similar material if clearing is impractical, leaving a path at least 36 inches wide. Commercial properties (or buildings with ground-floor commercial units) that abut an accessible curb ramp must also clear that ramp for direct access to the crosswalk. Violations are fined under Sec. 1-11, and if you don't do it, the superintendent of highways may clear it at your expense and place a lien on your property (Sec. 12-8(c)).

**Don't dump snow in the street (Sec. 12-9).** You may not push snow or ice from your land or building into a public way — unless it's broken up and spread evenly no more than three inches thick, during daylight, and only when the day's mean temperature is above 40°F. Violations are fined under Sec. 1-11.

**Snow emergency parking (Sec. 10-10, and towing under Sec. 11-2).** When the Commissioner of Public Works (or the Highway Superintendent acting in his stead) declares a snow emergency and announces it through news/media, parking is banned on one side of the street. The rule alternates by year: in a winter beginning in an odd year, the even-address side is off-limits; in a winter beginning in an even year, the odd-address side is off-limits. Where parking is already banned on one side at all times, that stays unchanged. Vehicles that obstruct plowing can be towed, with towing and storage costs charged to the owner (Sec. 11-2); current fees are on file with the city clerk. Commercial vehicles briefly loading/unloading are exempt.

**Gotchas.** A few streets have special rules listed in Schedule T (currently the odd side of Crocker St's entire length) — approved streets restrict parking to the side with houses in both park-even and park-odd seasons. The provided sections don't specify the exact fine amounts (they refer you to Sec. 1-11 and the city clerk's office). For related towing rules, see Sec. 9-92 on abandoned vehicles.
