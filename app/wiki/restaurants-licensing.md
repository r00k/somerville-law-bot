Opening a restaurant or food business in Somerville touches several parts of the code. To operate as an innholder or common victualler (a traditional term for a food/lodging seller), you need a license from the licensing commission (Sec. 2-218). Before the commission decides on a new license, a transfer of ownership, or a change of location, it must forward copies of your application to the city council, council members' homes, and the police chief at least three weeks ahead, with agenda notice timelines spelled out in subsections (b) and (c).

**Operating hours.** No retail, restaurant, or service business open to the public may stay open between midnight and 5:00 a.m. without an extended-hours permit from the city council (Sec. 8-7(a)). Note the definitions in (b): "restaurant" here covers prepared-food sales for on- or off-premises consumption but *excludes* establishments licensed to serve alcohol. Business hours must be posted on your window or door (8-7(c)). Extended-hours permits expire each May 15 and may require a public hearing with certified-mail notice to property owners within 300 feet (8-7(d)–(f)). Each day open in violation is a separate offense (8-7(i), (k)).

**Zoning use categories.** Under the zoning ordinance, a "Bar, Restaurant, or Food Hall" (§ 9.2.7.a) serves ready-to-consume food/drink plus alcohol for on-site consumption and requires a City Council license to serve alcohol; outdoor seating is allowed as an ancillary use. A "Cafe or Coffee Shop" (§ 9.2.7.b) serves food/drink without alcohol. Confirm your location's zoning district permits your chosen use.

**Grease and outdoor dogs.** Food establishments must store cooking grease in impervious, tightly covered containers subject to health-department inspection, clean up spillage, and contract with a licensed scavenger for removal (Sec. 11-36); violations run $50–$300 per day. Restaurants wanting to allow dogs in outdoor seating are governed by board of health regulations (Sec. 6-24).

**Gaps:** These sections do not lay out general food-safety inspection standards, kitchen requirements, or the full alcohol-licensing process (governed largely by state law). Check with the licensing commission and health department for those specifics.
