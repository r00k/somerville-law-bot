Somerville treats graffiti as a public nuisance (Sec. 9-59) and defines it broadly as any letters, words, drawings, carvings, etchings, or other markings — not required or approved by law — that deface or damage real property (Sec. 9-60). "Real property" includes not just buildings but poles, fences, fire hydrants, gravestones, trees, and rocks, whether publicly or privately owned.

Property owners are responsible for prompt removal of graffiti once they discover it or receive notice (Sec. 9-61). For private property, the Board of Health (or its agent) serves a written "order of notice" describing the graffiti and its location, and the owner then has ten days to remove it (Sec. 9-61(b)). "Owner" is defined broadly to include titleholders, agents, trustees, mortgagees in possession, and others with care or control of the property (Sec. 9-60).

If you disagree with an order, you can request a hearing before the Board of Health by filing a written petition within seven days of service (Sec. 9-62); the hearing must begin within 30 days. After the hearing the Board may sustain, modify, or withdraw the order (Sec. 9-62(c)), and an aggrieved owner may then appeal to court (Sec. 9-63).

Gotcha: If you do not request a hearing within seven days and fail to remove the graffiti in the allotted time — or fail to comply within ten days after a hearing — the city may enter your property and remove the graffiti itself (Sec. 9-64), and you may face the cost and penalty provisions referenced in the notice (Secs. 9-64, 9-66, 9-67).

Separately, it is illegal to deface property or write obscene/indecent words on structures in public places (Sec. 9-47), to vandalize public property (Sec. 9-46), and to mark subways or public passageways (Sec. 9-107). Stores may not sell or give spray paint or indelible markers to anyone under 18 (Sec. 9-93). The provided sections do not state the specific dollar amount of removal costs or fines — those are set by the referenced penalty sections (e.g., 9-66, 9-67, 1-11), which are not included here.
